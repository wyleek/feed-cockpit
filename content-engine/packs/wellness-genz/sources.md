# Sources & tiers: Wellness × Gen Z

Discovery is WIDE-NET, not a fixed feed list. This file gives the engine (1) what to search for, (2) the tier rubric the vetting layer applies to whatever it catches, and (3) the red flags that auto-reject. The engine can pull a story from an outlet listed nowhere here — vetting decides if it's trustworthy.

## Tier rubric (the vetting layer applies this to EVERY catch — see engine/source-vetting.md)

Tiers judge the **origin** of a claim, not whoever wrote it up.

- **Tier 1 — Primary/authoritative.** Peer-reviewed journals (JAMA, Lancet, Nature, BMJ, Cell, Science, PNAS, etc.), NIH/NIMH/CDC/WHO, major university research offices — verified against the actual study, journal confirmed non-predatory. Can support any factual claim.
- **Tier 2 — Quality health journalism.** Bylined outlets with editorial standards that link their sources (NPR, STAT, AP, Guardian, Vox, university press, EurekAlert/ScienceDaily as press-release relays). Trace to the Tier 1 origin and cite that. Press releases oversell — always check the study.
- **Tier 3 — Trend/culture.** TikTok trends, brand surveys, influencer claims, culture mags. Can ONLY support "this is trending" framing — never a factual health claim alone. Note: Tier 3 is where the best STORY IDEAS start ("everyone's doing X — does it work?"); the receipts then come from Tiers 1–2.

## Optional anchor sources (a reliable FLOOR on a thin day — NOT a ceiling)

Check these in addition to the net, never instead of it. If one 404s twice, note it under "Feed health" and move on.
- NIH / NIMH / CDC newsrooms (Tier 1)
- EurekAlert + ScienceDaily, Mind & Brain / Health (Tier 2 relays — verify the study)
- NPR Health "Shots", STAT, Guardian Health (Tier 2)

## Red flags (vetting auto-rejects)

- Predatory / pay-to-publish journals (unfamiliar journal → check it)
- Supplement/app/brand-funded studies without independent replication
- "Study" that's actually a brand's customer survey
- Content farms / AI-generated SEO pages (topic-salad articles, no author, programmatic phrasing — e.g. a "mental health" page splicing in sports-betting or crypto copy)
- Single-influencer claims with no underlying source
- Claims the origin source doesn't actually support
- Duty-of-care topics per pack.md unless explicitly cleared by the user
